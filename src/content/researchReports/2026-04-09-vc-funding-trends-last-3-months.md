## Executive summary

The last three months produced one of the most consequential—and most misleading—venture quarters in recent memory.

On the surface, the numbers look euphoric. Global startup funding reached roughly **$297 billion in Q1 2026**, the highest quarterly total on record, according to Crunchbase data reported by TechCrunch.[^1] But that record was not driven by a broad-based rebound. It was driven by an extraordinary concentration of capital into a tiny number of companies.

The clearest proof: **OpenAI ($122B), Anthropic ($30B), xAI ($20B), and Waymo ($16B)** together accounted for about **$188 billion**, or more than **63% of all global Q1 funding**.[^1]

That makes the real story of the quarter more precise than the headline "venture is back."

The more accurate reading is this:

- **Late-stage venture has reopened for a narrow set of frontier-scale winners**, especially in AI model labs, compute infrastructure, robotics, and autonomy.
- **Early-stage venture is still moving, but with more discipline**, as more dollars flow to fewer companies.[^2][^3][^4]
- **Seed is alive, but deeply bifurcated**: elite AI teams and infrastructure-adjacent companies are raising larger rounds at richer valuations, while non-consensus and non-AI startups face slower fundraising and tougher scrutiny.[^1][^5][^6]
- **Series A remains the operational center of the market**: meaningful capital is available for companies with traction, but investor discipline around GTM quality, efficiency, and technical differentiation has hardened.[^4]
- **Series B and beyond are working again—but selectively**, especially for companies tied to AI infrastructure, defense, legal tech, healthcare workflows, fintech infrastructure, robotics, semiconductors, and industrial or sovereign infrastructure.[^3][^7][^8][^9]

For founders, the implication is straightforward: **this is not a uniformly strong funding market. It is a highly concentrated one**.

Startups inside today’s capital magnets—frontier AI, AI infrastructure, robotics, hard tech, defense, semiconductors, applied workflow software, and regulated financial infrastructure—can still raise meaningful rounds. Companies outside those zones can also raise, but the burden of proof is materially higher.

## The big picture: a record quarter shaped by concentration

### Global capital surged—but the breadth did not

Crunchbase data reported by TechCrunch shows **global startup funding hit $297 billion in Q1 2026**, up about **2.5x from Q4 2025’s $118 billion**.[^1] That single quarter exceeded the full-year global VC total of every year prior to 2019.[^1]

That sounds like a broad venture recovery. It was not.

The four largest rounds of the quarter were:

- **OpenAI** — **$122 billion**, at an **$852 billion valuation**[^1]
- **Anthropic** — **$30 billion Series G**, at a **$380 billion post-money valuation**[^10]
- **xAI** — **$20 billion Series E**[^1]
- **Waymo** — **$16 billion**[^1]

Those four financings alone totaled **$188 billion**. In other words, nearly two-thirds of all Q1 startup funding globally went into just four companies.[^1]

That is the defining signal of the last three months: **the market did not broaden; it concentrated**.

### AI is no longer just a sector—it is the market’s pacing force

A related Crunchbase-based analysis put AI companies at roughly **$242 billion, or about 80%-81% of all Q1 2026 venture funding**.[^11][^12] Even allowing for category noise, the directional conclusion is unmistakable: the current market is being priced, paced, and psychologically anchored by AI.

That shift matters because it changes how nearly every category is being evaluated:

- Venture is no longer being led primarily by traditional SaaS application software.
- The center of gravity has moved toward **models, compute, data-center infrastructure, semiconductors, robotics, autonomous systems, and AI-native workflow software**.
- Traditional categories are increasingly judged through an AI lens—either they benefit from AI, enable AI, or compete for a smaller pool of non-AI capital.

### Record dollars did not translate into record deal volume

The same pattern showed up in North America. Crunchbase reports that U.S. and Canadian companies raised **$252.6 billion** in seed-through-growth funding in Q1 2026, the highest quarterly total on record, with **more than 87% of that capital going to AI-related categories**.[^3]

But deal counts did not keep pace. Crunchbase notes that gains in late-stage and growth were driven by **larger deals, not more deals**.[^3] At seed, **round counts fell sequentially and year over year** even while dollar volume remained historically strong.[^3]

That is the core pattern founders should keep in mind:

> **More money. Fewer winners. Bigger checks. Higher selectivity.**

## Where the money is actually going

## 1) Frontier AI labs and model companies

This remains the market’s single biggest destination for capital.

Anthropic’s February financing is the clearest example after OpenAI. The company announced a **$30 billion Series G** at a **$380 billion post-money valuation**, with the capital earmarked for frontier research, product development, and infrastructure expansion.[^10]

The investor message is unmistakable: frontier AI is now being financed less like conventional venture software and more like strategic infrastructure.

**Why it is getting funded:**
- Massive demand for model capacity and inference
- Strategic importance to cloud, hardware, and enterprise ecosystems
- Investor belief that a small number of platforms will capture outsized value

**What it means for founders:** unless a startup is genuinely competing at frontier-model scale, the takeaway is not "raise like Anthropic." The takeaway is that **investors increasingly want companies that plug into, enable, or defensibly exploit the frontier-model stack**.

## 2) AI infrastructure, data centers, networking, semiconductors, and hyperscale plumbing

If frontier labs are the headline, infrastructure is the second major capital magnet.

Notable examples from the last three months include:

- **Nscale** raised **$2 billion Series C** at a **$14.6 billion valuation** to expand vertically integrated AI infrastructure across Europe, North America, and Asia.[^13]
- **Nscale** also secured a **$1.4 billion delayed draw term loan** in February for AI infrastructure deployment across Norway, Portugal, Iceland, and the UK.[^14]
- **Nexthop AI** raised an oversubscribed **$500 million Series B** at a **$4.2 billion valuation**, led by Lightspeed with participation from Andreessen Horowitz and Altimeter, to build AI networking infrastructure for hyperscalers and neo-clouds.[^15]
- **Apptronik** reopened and expanded its Series A, adding **$520 million** and bringing total Series A funding to **more than $935 million** to scale humanoid robotics production and deployment.[^8][^16]

This is not just spillover from the AI boom. It is a structural shift. Infrastructure is no longer sitting behind the software cycle. It is one of the primary investment theses.

**Why it is getting funded:**
- AI workloads are exposing real physical bottlenecks: GPUs, power, cooling, networking, storage, and orchestration
- The buyer set includes hyperscalers, governments, defense programs, and large enterprises
- Investors see the infrastructure layer as capable of supporting very large outcomes and durable moats

**What it means for founders:** "picks and shovels" remains highly investable—but only if the product solves a real bottleneck. Investors are not paying up for generic tooling. They are paying up for scarcity reduction.

## 3) Robotics, autonomy, defense, and embodied AI

The last three months also reinforced investor appetite for robotics and autonomy, especially where the use case is industrial, logistics-driven, defense-related, or tied to labor substitution.

Key examples:

- **Apptronik** added **$520 million** to its Series A in February, with backing from Google, Mercedes-Benz, B Capital, AT&T Ventures, John Deere, and Qatar Investment Authority, bringing total Series A funding to **more than $935 million**.[^8][^16]
- Crunchbase identified **Apptronik’s $520 million Series A** as the largest early-stage round in North America in Q1.[^3]
- Crunchbase also highlighted multiple other **$500 million early-stage financings** in Q1, including **Nexthop AI**, **MatX**, and **Mind Robotics**.[^3]
- **Shield AI** led the late-stage field in March with a **$2.0 billion Series G**, according to The State of Venture’s March report.[^4]

Robotics now sits at the intersection of AI capability, labor economics, sovereign urgency, and physical-world automation. That is a powerful combination in the current market.

**Why it is getting funded:**
- Strong sovereign and industrial demand signals
- AI progress makes robotics feel closer to commercialization
- Hardware-heavy categories are benefiting from the broader return of hard-tech investing

**What it means for founders:** the category is open, but the bar is high. The market is funding companies with credible deployment paths—not just compelling demos.

## 4) Applied AI software: legal, healthcare, workflow, and enterprise tooling

Not all of the capital is going into model labs and infrastructure. There is also strong investor demand for software companies using AI to improve high-value workflows.

Examples from the last three months include:

- **Legora**, the Swedish legal AI company, raised **$550 million Series D** at a **$5.55 billion valuation** to expand in the U.S. The round was led by Accel with participation from Benchmark, Bessemer, General Catalyst, ICONIQ, Redpoint, Y Combinator, and others.[^7][^17]
- In digital health, Rock Health reported **$4.0 billion across 110 deals in Q1 2026**, with capital concentrating heavily at the top and AI becoming so pervasive that Rock Health is retiring "AI deal" as a separate tracking category.[^9]
- Rock Health’s top Q1 rounds included **Verily ($300M)**, **Talkiatry ($210M Series D)**, **eMed ($200M Series A)**, **Grow Therapy ($150M Series D)**, **Honest Health ($140M)**, **Solace ($130M Series C)**, **Qualified Health ($125M)**, **Garner Health ($118M Series D)**, **Cognito Therapeutics ($105M)**, and **Midi Health ($100M Series D)**.[^9]

The pattern here is important. Applied AI is investable when it is tied to workflow ownership, distribution, compliance, or measurable ROI. A generic "AI wrapper" story is not enough. Investors are backing companies embedded inside economically meaningful systems.

**Why it is getting funded:**
- AI can improve productivity in expensive, high-friction knowledge workflows
- Enterprise ROI is easier to underwrite than speculative consumer AI adoption
- Vertical software with data and workflow integration looks more defensible than generic model access

**What it means for founders:** the best software pitch in this market is no longer "we use AI." It is: **we own a painful workflow, improve the economics, and can prove retention and expansion**.

## 5) Fintech infrastructure and stablecoin payments

Fintech is no longer the broad venture darling it was in 2020 and 2021, but the last three months show there is still meaningful appetite for **infrastructure-oriented fintech**—especially stablecoin-powered and cross-border payment rails.

Examples:

- **Rain** raised **$250 million Series C** on January 9 at a **$1.95 billion valuation**, led by ICONIQ, to scale stablecoin-powered payments infrastructure globally.[^18]
- **OpenFX** raised **$94 million** in March, with Reuters reporting a valuation of about **$500 million**. The company said it processes **more than $45 billion in annualized payment volume**, up from **$4 billion a year earlier**, and that **more than 98% of transactions settle in under 60 minutes**.[^19]

This is a useful market signal. Broad consumer-fintech narratives remain harder to finance, but infrastructure that lowers costs and accelerates money movement is still attracting capital.

**Why it is getting funded:**
- Payments and FX remain enormous cost centers
- Stablecoins are increasingly being framed as plumbing, not speculation
- Enterprise adoption is a stronger underwriting story than retail crypto exposure

**What it means for founders:** fintech can still raise, but today’s appetite is strongest for infrastructure, compliance, and cost-saving rails—not broad consumer narratives.

## 6) Healthcare and digital health

Healthcare was not the quarter’s dominant story, but it remained a meaningful funding market—and one with the same structural pattern seen elsewhere: larger checks going to later-stage winners, with AI increasingly assumed rather than treated as novel.

Rock Health found:

- **$4.0 billion** in Q1 digital health funding across **110 deals**[^9]
- Up from **$3.0 billion across 122 deals** in Q1 2025[^9]
- **Average deal size rose to $36.7 million**, the highest since Q4 2021[^9]
- Roughly **59%-60% of capital** went into **12 mega-deals**[^9][^20]

Healthcare is therefore following the broader venture pattern closely: **selective growth, top-end concentration, and AI normalized rather than differentiated**.

**What it means for founders:** healthcare companies can still raise if they sit close to reimbursement, enterprise budgets, workflow integration, or meaningful consumer behavior change backed by strong distribution. Purely conceptual health-AI narratives will struggle.

## Geography: where capital concentrated

## United States and North America: still the clear center of gravity

North America captured the vast majority of the quarter’s funding momentum.

Crunchbase’s Q1 North America review shows:

- **$252.6 billion** raised by U.S. and Canadian companies in Q1[^3]
- **More than 87%** of that capital went to AI-related categories[^3]
- **$222.4 billion**, or **88%**, went to late-stage and technology-growth rounds[^3]
- **$25.1 billion** went to early-stage rounds (roughly Series A and B), up **17% sequentially** and **56% year over year**[^3]
- **$5.1 billion** went to seed and pre-seed, roughly flat sequentially[^3]

The conclusion is clear: the U.S. remains the default home of frontier AI and the primary market for outsized venture deployment.

## Europe: real momentum, especially in infrastructure and elite AI teams

Europe did not match the U.S. in absolute dollars, but it produced several of the quarter’s most important non-U.S. financings.

Highlights include:

- **Nscale** raised **$2 billion Series C**, described as the largest Series C in European history for an AI infrastructure company.[^13]
- A Financial Times-reported deal saw **Yann LeCun’s new Paris AI startup** raise **more than $1 billion in Europe’s largest seed round**, a striking sign that elite AI talent can now raise capital at a scale previously reserved for late-stage companies.[^21]
- **Legora** raised **$550 million Series D** as legal AI adoption accelerated among U.S. and global law firms.[^7][^17]

Europe’s signal is important: while it still trails the U.S. in frontier-scale concentration, it is producing major financings in infrastructure, legal AI, and top-decile research talent.

## China: state-backed hard-tech funding accelerated

China’s Q1 story looked different from the U.S. model. Reuters reported that fundraising in China’s VC industry was on pace to hit a **quarterly record**, driven by **state-backed capital**, especially in AI, robotics, and hard tech.[^22]

Key details from Reuters:

- Newly committed capital to Chinese venture funds totaled **86 billion yuan ($12.51 billion)** in the first two months of 2026[^22]
- That pace was set to exceed the prior quarterly record of **68.9 billion yuan** from Q3 2021[^22]
- Nearly all of the top investors in roughly **1,200 new yuan-denominated VC funds** formed in Q1 were government entities or state-owned firms[^22]
- Reuters highlighted investment interest in **AI, robotics, moon exploration, brain-computer interface, quantum computing, and semiconductors**[^22]

For founders and investors, China reinforces an emerging global pattern: **strategically important technologies are increasingly being financed by policy-shaped capital, not just market-driven venture capital**.

## Stage-by-stage implications for entrepreneurs

## Pre-seed

### What the market looks like

Pre-seed remains open, but it is not easy money. The State of Venture’s March 2026 report counted **81 pre-seed rounds** totaling **$153.1 million**, with a **$1.0 million median round size**.[^4]

That is a healthy level of activity. But it also shows that the median pre-seed round remains modest. The market is still funding company formation—not indiscriminate experimentation.

### What is getting funded

- Deeply technical founding teams, especially in AI, robotics, infrastructure, semiconductors, and security
- Founders with unusual credibility or prior wins
- Startups positioned around clear future bottlenecks rather than broad idea spaces

### What it means for founders

At pre-seed, narrative still matters—but in 2026 it needs to be a **technical and market-specific narrative**, not just a broad vision deck.

Founders should assume investors want:
- A crisp wedge
- Unusually strong founder-market fit
- Evidence of technical speed
- A plausible path to data advantage, workflow control, or infrastructure leverage

The practical lesson is simple: **pre-seed is available, but the market increasingly rewards exceptional founder quality over market breadth**.

## Seed

### What the market looks like

Seed remains the most active stage by number of rounds. In March alone, The State of Venture counted **261 seed rounds totaling $2.6 billion**, with a **$4.1 million median seed round**.[^4]

But broader data suggests a bifurcated market:

- TechCrunch reported founders and investors saying **seed-stage AI startups are commanding bigger dollars and higher valuations at earlier stages than ever before**.[^1]
- Crunchbase’s North America Q1 report said **seed and pre-seed investment totaled $5.1 billion**, roughly flat sequentially, while **seed round counts declined** both sequentially and year over year.[^3]
- Carta benchmarks cited in 2026 reporting indicate the **median seed round in 2025 was about $4.0 million**, with AI software companies carrying a clear valuation premium.[^5]

### What is getting funded

- AI-native products with strong technical teams
- Tools embedded in enterprise workflows
- Developer tools, infrastructure, and security
- Robotics and applied hard tech
- Financial infrastructure, especially stablecoin and payments plumbing

### What it means for founders

Seed is no longer just about a promising market and a strong team. Increasingly, it is about one of three heat sources:

1. **Category heat** — you are operating in an area investors already want exposure to
2. **Team heat** — your team is unusually credible
3. **Traction heat** — you are already showing meaningful pull

If a startup has none of those, fundraising will likely be difficult.

For seed founders outside AI, the burden is to show at least one of the following:
- Unusual customer urgency
- Capital efficiency
- A strong distribution advantage
- A regulatory, workflow, or data moat

## Series A

### What the market looks like

Series A remains the stage where the market is active—but disciplined.

The State of Venture’s March 2026 report found **158 Series A rounds totaling $5.0 billion**, with a **$17.3 million median round**.[^4]

Crunchbase’s Q1 North America review shows early-stage capital—covering roughly Series A and B—at **$25.1 billion**, up **17%** from the prior quarter and **56% year over year**, though round counts were down somewhat.[^3]

That is the right way to interpret the Series A market today:
- There is real money available
- Pricing can still be attractive for top companies
- Investors remain selective and increasingly concentrated

### What is getting funded

- AI infrastructure and tooling
- Enterprise AI with strong workflow lock-in
- Robotics, chips, autonomy, and defense-related platforms
- Healthcare and fintech infrastructure companies with measurable customer value

Examples from Q1 include large early-stage rounds such as **Apptronik’s $520M Series A extension** and multiple **$500M early-stage financings** like Nexthop AI, MatX, and Mind Robotics.[^3][^8]

### What it means for founders

For most startups, Series A is now the stage where a good story stops being enough. Investors want:

- Repeatable customer demand
- Evidence that the product is sticky
- Some proof of an efficient GTM motion
- Clearer category leadership potential

Founders entering Series A should expect scrutiny on:
- Revenue quality
- Gross margin trajectory
- Customer concentration
- Retention and expansion behavior
- Depth of technical edge

The good news: **Series A is open**. The harder truth: it increasingly behaves like a quality filter rather than a default milestone financing.

## Series B

### What the market looks like

Series B is clearly healthier than it was during the post-2022 freeze, but it is still not broad-based.

The State of Venture’s March report counted **78 Series B rounds totaling $4.3 billion**, with a **$34.9 million median round**.[^4]

Carta’s 2024 review showed that Series B had already begun recovering before this quarter, with capital and deal activity improving versus 2023 and median valuations rising.[^6] Q1 2026 suggests that recovery continued—but mostly in favored sectors.

### What is getting funded

- AI infrastructure
- High-growth enterprise AI
- Fintech infrastructure
- Companies with clear category leadership and durable demand

Specific March examples cited by The State of Venture include **Nexthop AI ($500M Series B)** and **Cloaked ($375M Series B)** anchoring the largest B rounds for the month.[^4]

### What it means for founders

At Series B, investors are underwriting more than product-market fit. They are underwriting **scale quality**.

They want to know:

- Is growth durable or opportunistic?
- Is AI improving margins or only increasing burn?
- Can the company become an enduring category leader?
- Is the market large enough to support a truly large outcome?

Series B founders should expect diligence to focus heavily on unit economics, market leadership, and whether the company can keep growing without serial capital dependency.

## Series C, D, and beyond

### What the market looks like

This is where the last three months were strongest.

The State of Venture’s March 2026 report found:

- **42 Series C rounds totaling $3.1 billion**, with a **$44.5 million median**[^4]
- **23 rounds across Series D through G totaling $5.2 billion**[^4]
- **Shield AI’s $2.0 billion Series G** as the largest late-stage March deal[^4]

At the quarter level, Crunchbase shows late-stage and growth massively dominating capital deployment in North America, with **$222.4 billion** invested at those stages.[^3]

### What is getting funded

- Frontier AI labs
- AI compute and infrastructure
- Autonomy and defense
- Scaled applied AI platforms
- Category leaders with very large market narratives

Examples include **Anthropic**, **Waymo**, **Shield AI**, **Legora**, major digital health winners, and large AI infrastructure companies.[^7][^9][^10]

### What it means for founders

Late-stage financing is back for companies that can credibly tell one of three stories:

1. **We are a platform winner in a category investors believe can be massive**
2. **We are indispensable infrastructure in the AI economy**
3. **We have the revenue and category position to justify scale capital despite a still-limited exit market**

For everyone else, late stage remains selective. The market has reopened, but it has not become forgiving.

## What investors are doing differently

The last three months point to several investor behaviors that founders should pay close attention to.

### 1) Fewer but larger bets

Across sectors and stages, the same pattern keeps repeating: more dollars are being deployed into fewer companies.

- Crunchbase saw this in North America across late-stage and seed.[^3]
- Rock Health saw it in digital health, where 12 mega-deals captured nearly 60% of Q1 funding.[^9][^20]
- The State of Venture’s March report found the **top 10 deals accounted for 52.1% of all capital** deployed that month.[^4]

### 2) Elite investors are clustering around obvious winners

The biggest rounds repeatedly featured the same categories of capital:

- Major multi-stage firms: **Accel, Andreessen Horowitz, Benchmark, Bessemer, General Catalyst, Lightspeed**
- Large crossover or growth investors: **Altimeter, ICONIQ, Point72, Sands Capital**
- Strategic and ecosystem players: **Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, Dell, Mercedes-Benz**
- Sovereign or state-backed capital in both Europe and China[^7][^8][^13][^22]

This increases competition at the top end of the market while leaving the middle more rational—but also more demanding.

### 3) Technical credibility is commanding a premium

The billion-dollar European seed round around Yann LeCun’s new startup is the clearest example.[^21] Investors are paying huge premiums for founder quality where they believe a team can plausibly build category-defining technical advantage.

That logic is not new. The magnitude is.

## What all of this means for founders right now

## 1) Funding is back—but only for certain companies

Founders should stop asking whether funding is back in general and start asking a more useful question: **for whom is funding back?**

Over the last three months, the answer has been:

- Very much yes for frontier AI and AI infrastructure
- Yes for elite applied AI companies in high-value workflows
- Yes for robotics, autonomy, defense, semiconductors, and select hard-tech plays
- Yes for healthcare and fintech infrastructure companies with strong proof points
- Mixed to difficult for most other categories

## 2) In some markets, AI is now table stakes rather than differentiation

In healthcare, Rock Health has effectively declared AI too pervasive to track as a separate deal type.[^9] That is a useful heuristic beyond healthcare as well.

For many startups, AI is no longer the wedge by itself. The real wedge must be one of the following:

- Distribution
- Workflow ownership
- Proprietary data
- Operational leverage
- Regulatory advantage
- Hardware or infrastructure moat

## 3) Stage still matters—but sector and founder quality matter more than they used to

Pre-seed and seed are still alive, but the variance has widened dramatically. Exceptional teams in hot categories can raise on extraordinary terms. Average teams in average categories will struggle, regardless of stage.

## 4) Specificity is now a fundraising advantage

Generalist pitches are weak in a concentrated market. The strongest companies are telling investors exactly:

- What bottleneck they solve
- Why that bottleneck matters now
- Why they are uniquely suited to solve it
- Why the timing is unusually good

## 5) Credibility beats theater

As investors concentrate capital, they spend more time on fewer opportunities. That dynamic favors rigorous founders.

The market is currently rewarding:

- Clear technical substance
- Sober metrics
- Strong customer references
- Realistic capital planning
- Category-specific insight

It is punishing:

- Vague AI branding
- Undifferentiated SaaS claims
- Growth without quality
- High burn without a compelling strategic reason

## Final assessment

The last three months did not mark a normal venture recovery. They marked the consolidation of a **new capital regime**.

That regime has five defining traits:

1. **Extreme concentration** — record dollars, but heavily skewed toward a few companies and sectors[^1][^4]
2. **AI dominance** — frontier AI and AI-adjacent infrastructure are setting the pace for the rest of venture[^1][^10][^13]
3. **Selective early-stage health** — seed and Series A remain active, but fewer companies are getting funded and the bar is higher[^3][^4]
4. **Late-stage reopening** — especially for infrastructure, robotics, defense, healthcare winners, legal AI, and scaled workflow platforms[^7][^9][^13]
5. **A widening gap between winners and everyone else** — quality, credibility, and category position matter more than ever[^9][^22]

For entrepreneurs, the takeaway is not pessimistic. Capital is still available—and in some categories, it is abundant. But this is now a market where **clarity beats charisma, bottleneck-solving beats broad ambition, and technical or commercial proof beats narrative inflation**.

That is the real funding trend of the last three months.
